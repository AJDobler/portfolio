An interactive dinner game to help break the barrier to conversation
Project Goal: Our team was tasked with creating an experience using tangible user interfaces in any way we wanted.
Team Size: 7 People
Project Length: 3 Weeks
Problem Space: After watching this video, we got inspired to work within the dining experience and see what we could do with an interactive table. We wanted to make it easier for groups to bond easily with each other through light-hearted conversation - especially those where everyone didn’t know each other too well.

Our team initially focused on altering the board game experience to use tangible user interfaces, but we felt trying to force technology into something like a board game would detract from the experience that makes a board game special - the physical aspects and lack of technology.
Final Design
Our improved dining experience consists of two games that guests can play at the table. The animation game gets played during phase 2 and the conversation game gets played during phase 3.

Animation Game
How it works: This is inspired by the game We’re Not Really Strangers, which is a card game with discussion prompts that spark conversation and connect. Basically, we modified that style of game to work on a tangible table rather than using cards. How this would work is that the user lifts their napkin to reveal a conversation prompt on the table beneath it. If they feel uncomfortable answering the questions or would simply prefer a different one, they can put their napkin back on the table and pick it up again to refresh the prompt
Table space for drawing in front of each seating position
A drawing prompt that appears after guests order their meals
People draw with their forks and pass it to the next person
Why it works: The animation game serves as a natural icebreaker. Because this game is meant for people that might not all know each other, conversation gets focused on the game itself rather than awkward small talk such as asking how people’s days have been and talking about themselves.

Everyone can put their own unique twist on the game when the frame gets to them, and with it being easy to participate, people have a sense of belonging. Because this is a fun activity, dinner guests are warmed up to each other for our next game which is more serious and involves deeper conversation.
Conversation Game
How it works: This is inspired by the game We’re Not Really Strangers, which is a card game with discussion prompts that spark conversation and connect. Basically, we modified that style of game to work on a tangible table rather than using cards. How this would work is that the user lifts their napkin to reveal a conversation prompt on the table beneath it. If they feel uncomfortable answering the questions or would simply prefer a different one, they can put their napkin back on the table and pick it up again to refresh the prompt
Why it works: Since the dinner guests will feel a little more comfortable around each other through the first activity, we hope this game will strengthen those connections by sparking unexpected conversations and build relationships with one another through prompted questions and activities. Also, elevating mealtime activities is a way to mutually unite and create a sense of belonging within a community (Oxford, 2017).
Evaluation
To test our dining experience concept, we created a mock restaurant in the classroom and simulated it with the following methods:
  • Furniture to make a table booth
  • Paper to replicate napkins
  • Procreate animation feature on iPad to replicate animation game
  • Team member to act as waitress and explain games/answer questions
Findings
  • Drawing allowed participants to be distracted from the 'initial awkwardness' and bond over what they had created
  • Certain question prompts could take up more time and lengthen out the entire dinner
  • We need to consider those who may not want to talk during dinner and would rather spend time eating than talking
  • The game goes a lot smoother if there's at least one person decent at drawing to start the game off in a usable direction

Defining Stages in the Dining Experience
To orient our designs within the context of a meal, our team outlined the different phases of the dining experience. These helped make sure that our two games wouldn't feel too out of place or alter the the meal too much to be uncomfortable.
Phase 1
We titled this as the settling phase and agreed there is little discussion outside of what you are ordering.
Phase 2
While ordering food, you are busy looking through the menu and potentially asking advice on what food to try. However, once you order food, you are waiting with members of your party and have to participate in conversation - usually small talk - which can be awkward for group of unfamiliar people.
Phase 3
This phase serves somewhat as an extension to phase 3 but now with food. There are also people who finish their meals sooner than others which can make them feel like they're just waiting on others, or make the people still eating like they need to hurry up.
Phase 4
In this phase, everyone pays for their meals and finishes up discussions while they prepare to leave the restaurant.
Reflection
Most of the time we end up designing for a set user group that has certain characteristics, but this time we designed for a certain context that really could fit with anyone which made it engaging to design for. Usually in our program, we do a lot of testing with other students when our user group isn't anything close to UX students. This contrast made our testing results much more meaningful because it felt like we actually designed something interesting and the group of four we tested with genuinely enjoyed it.


This was also the first project I've worked on where our outcome was just making something that was fun. We work on so many projects where our goal is to solve some type of problem which made it nice to change it up. After all, one of my making design fun, so this was project was super fulfilling to see potential in.
